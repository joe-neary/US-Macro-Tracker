Methodology

US Economic Indicators Tracker - Technical Documentation

1. Overview

The tracker monitors {{ data.indicators|length if data and data.indicators else 29 }} macroeconomic indicators across 5 categories (Job Market, Inflation, Economic Activities, Leading Indicators, and Market Positioning). It combines three quantitative models to classify the current economic regime and produce a forward-looking assessment:

  • Dynamic Factor Model (DFM) - extracts latent growth, inflation, and credit factors from panel data
  • Hidden Markov Model (HMM) - probabilistic regime classification across 4 states
  • Weighted Macro Score - continuous scoring of all indicators with timing-based weights

2. Indicator Coverage

Data is sourced from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) API for 25 indicators, with 4 additional PMI indicators from PDF reports and CSV files. Each indicator is classified as:

TypeWeight RangeDescription
Leading2.0 - 3.0xPredicts future conditions 3-12 months ahead (credit spreads, yield curve, permits, JOLTS, EPU, DXY, housing starts)
Coincident1.5 - 2.0xMoves in real time (ISM PMIs, payrolls, ADP, claims, industrial production, retail sales, consumer sentiment)
Lagging1.0 - 2.0xConfirms what has happened (unemployment, CPI, Core CPI, Core PCE, PPI, CRB commodities)

3. Continuous Scoring

Each indicator is scored on a continuous scale from -1.0 (deeply contractionary) to +1.0 (deeply expansionary), based on its distance from a neutral midpoint:

score = clamp((value - midpoint) / scale, -1, +1) For inverted indicators (lower = better): score = clamp((midpoint - value) / scale, -1, +1) For goldilocks indicators (both extremes bad): score = clamp(-|value - midpoint| / scale, -1, 0)

This replaces the traditional discrete {-1, 0, +1} approach, allowing the weighted score to capture magnitude. CPI at 2.6% now scores differently from CPI at 8.0%.

4. Weighted Macro Score

The headline score is a weighted average of all continuous scores, grouped by timing tier:

score = SUM(continuous_score_i * weight_i) / SUM(weight_i)

Leading indicators receive 2-3x weight (they predict the future), coincident indicators 1.5-2x (they confirm the present), and lagging indicators 1-2x (they validate what happened). The output ranges from -1.0 to +1.0 with thresholds at +/-0.3 for expansionary/contractionary and +/-0.1 for mixed signals.

5. Dynamic Factor Model (DFM)

Three latent factors are extracted from the indicator panels using a state-space Dynamic Factor Model (Kalman filter via statsmodels). When the Kalman filter fails to converge, a PCA fallback is used.

Growth Factor

Estimated from: unemployment, initial claims, payrolls, ADP, consumer sentiment, CFNAI, building permits, JOLTS, industrial production, housing starts. Anchored to payrolls for sign normalisation.

Inflation Factor

Estimated from: CPI YoY, Core CPI YoY, Core PCE YoY, PPI MoM, CRB YoY, breakeven inflation, M2 money supply, import prices. Anchored to CPI.

Credit Factor

Estimated from: HY credit spread, IG credit spread, NFCI, 10Y-3M yield curve. Captures financial stress conditions.

Rolling z-score normalisation (HMM only)

The display DFM uses standard full-sample z-scoring. The HMM DFM uses a 120-month rolling z-score: each month's value is normalised against the trailing 10-year mean and standard deviation. This ensures that "elevated inflation" means "high relative to the recent decade" rather than "high relative to the last 50 years." Without this, the 1970s-80s inflation (CPI 8-14%) would pull the baseline up so much that current 2.4% CPI would appear deflationary.

The rolling approach lets the HMM train on 550+ months of regime dynamics (including the 1970s oil shocks and Volcker disinflation) while correctly classifying current conditions relative to modern norms.

6. Hidden Markov Model (HMM)

A 4-state Gaussian HMM is fitted to the (growth factor, inflation factor) pair using the Baum-Welch (EM) algorithm. The four states correspond to the standard investment clock quadrants:

RegimeGrowthInflationTypical Environment
Early RecoveryRisingFallingPost-recession, policy easing, disinflation
ReflationRisingRisingMid-cycle expansion, demand-pull inflation
StagflationFallingRisingSupply shocks, cost-push inflation, policy dilemma
DeflationFallingFallingRecession, demand collapse, disinflationary

"Rising/falling" is relative to the rolling 10-year trend, not absolute levels. The DFM z-scores determine whether each factor is above or below its recent baseline.

Implementation details

  • Training window: ~550 months (back to ~1975) with rolling 120-month z-score normalisation. Covers both 1970s oil shocks, Volcker disinflation, and all subsequent cycles.
  • Growth panel: 8 series - Unemployment, Claims, Payrolls, Consumer Sentiment, CFNAI, Building Permits, Industrial Production, Housing Starts
  • Inflation panel: 3 series - CPI YoY, Core CPI YoY, PPI MoM (Reuters commodity and Core PCE excluded from HMM to avoid collinearity)
  • Initialisation: State means placed at the 4 economic quadrants (+/-0.7 sigma), transition matrix starts at 90% persistence
  • Regularisation: 1% minimum transition probability (no absorbing states), 0.01 covariance floor (no spike collapse)
  • Convergence: EM stops when log-likelihood change < 0.0001
  • Model selection: Best of 6 random seeds by log-likelihood
  • State relabeling: After EM, states are reassigned to quadrants by dot-product alignment of fitted means with target vectors

Dual-model regime classification

Two regime views are computed and displayed side-by-side:

  • Full-sample (smoothed): Forward-backward probabilities using all available data. This is the "where we ended up" view - the best estimate of the true regime at each point in time.
  • Walk-forward (out-of-sample): Each month classified using only data available at that time (expanding-window Kalman DFM + HMM, pre-computed via --walk-forward CLI). No look-ahead bias.

Transition detection

When the two models disagree on the current regime, the economy is classified as in transition. The walk-forward shows where we ARE (real-time), the full-sample shows where we're HEADING (with hindsight). Transition is also flagged when Shannon entropy of the probability distribution exceeds a threshold (stability < 80%).

7. Directional Forecast

Compares each leading indicator's current value vs its value 3 months ago. If >60% of leading indicators are deteriorating (or >40% with DFM momentum confirming), the outlook is "DETERIORATING". The conviction signal combines HMM confidence with forecast direction, with a conflict check: if the HMM says growth-positive but leading indicators are weakening, conviction is downgraded. When regime stability is below 50%, conviction automatically overrides to "TRANSITION."

8. Regime Backtest

S&P 500 monthly returns (OECD index, FRED: SPASTT01USM661N) are overlaid on the HMM regime classifications. When a walk-forward cache exists, the backtest uses out-of-sample classifications (green "Out-of-sample" badge). Otherwise it falls back to in-sample filtered probabilities (amber "In-sample" badge). Per-regime statistics include average monthly return, annualised Sharpe ratio, and hit rate.

9. Data Quality

  • Traffic light freshness: Green (up to date), amber (old but latest available for this indicator), red (staler than expected - possible fetch error)
  • Anomaly detection: Values >4 sigma from recent 12-month history are flagged with a warning icon
  • PDF parsing: ISM Manufacturing, ISM Services, Chicago PMI, and S&P Global Services PMI are extracted from press release PDFs using regex pattern matching
  • Alert system: Regime changes, macro score shifts >0.15, and outlook changes are logged and displayed as dashboard banners

10. Limitations and Caveats

  • The rolling 120-month z-score means the inflation factor is sensitive to whether the trailing window includes abnormal periods (e.g., the 2021-22 spike). This is by design - "elevated" means "relative to the recent decade" - but can produce counterintuitive results when the recent decade itself was unusual.
  • The model cannot predict exogenous shocks (geopolitical events, pandemics, sudden policy changes). The EPU index provides partial warning but reacts to events rather than predicting them.
  • Continuous scoring midpoints and scales are judgment-based, not empirically optimised (validation available via --validate-params). They reflect conventional macro thresholds.
  • The walk-forward backtest retrains the Kalman DFM + HMM each month (genuine out-of-sample), but uses latest-revised FRED data rather than real-time vintages. Historical classifications may differ from what would have been computed in real time.
  • The walk-forward and full-sample models can disagree on the current regime. This is treated as a feature (transition detection) rather than a bug, but means the "primary" regime depends on which model you trust more.
  • The sector playbook uses the full-sample regime for allocation recommendations. During transitions, the probability-weighted portfolio allocation is more appropriate than the single-regime playbook.
